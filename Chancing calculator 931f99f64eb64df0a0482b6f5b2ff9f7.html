Chancing calculator: Transforming the experience of estimating college admission chances

Overview

I redesigned the CollegeVine chancing calculator, a tool that helps US high school students estimate their chances of being admitted to colleges.

My Role

Product designer; responsible for the end-to-end design process from discovery to UI design

The Team

1 Product Manager, 2 Engineers

What is the CollegeVine chancing calculator?

A chancing calculator estimates the chances of getting into each college for high school students in the US. It is one of the most searched college application tools on Google. CollegeVine has the most sophisticated chancing calculator on the market.

It is the main source of organic traffic for CollegeVine. 75% of new users come from admissions calculator landing pages.

The old workflow

New users sign up for CollegeVine, complete a wizard-based profile intake process, and see chances in the “explore colleges” page where they can search for colleges.

They then have a platform profile — a long input form — which they can review and edit.

The problems

This process works, but is far from ideal. In fact, from user interviews, screen recordings, and our knowledge about the platform, we identified many problems to be solved for the calculator. The prominent ones that impact multiple parts of the workflow are:

New users: Time to value is extremely long

The calculator requires users to input many data points, which can take 15–30 minutes, leading to significant drop-off in the onboarding phase.

Power users: They keep changing their platform profile to simulate chances

We observed from screen recordings that our power users keep changing their platform profile to see how it changes their chances. They needed to switch back and forth between pages to do that.

Chances are hard to understand

We get many questions about the accuracy of the calculator, and a lot of it comes from not understanding how it is calculated.

So…how might we re-imagine the chancing experience?

  • Make time-to-value extremely short
  • Make it super easy to simulate different profiles
  • Make it easier to understand chances

How we measure success

Retention is the North star metric we were trying to improve. We also identified the action of adding a school to the user’s college list as a leading indicator for retention.

We hypothesized that, since chancing is such a central part of the admissions process and the main source of traffic for our platform, retention would go up if we successfully improve the overall chancing experience for our users.

The business constraint

We want to collect “more-or-less” the same user profile data we’re collecting today. As an example, simplifying the algorithm and collecting less detailed data would not serve our needs.

Team brainstorming to involve engineers early

In order to get the thoughts of the whole team on how to improve this long-existing feature, I conducted a Crazy 8 style brainstorming session. We ventured into the then Beta version of FigJam. After making sure everyone is clear about current problems and our prompt, the team sketched away. With this method, we generated a list of improvements we wanted to work on.

Turning concepts into prioritized solutions

Walking away from the brainstorming, I turned the team-generated concepts into low to mid fidelity mockups to show stakeholders our vision.

It is not realistic to work on these many ideas at the same time, so I used the assumption grid to prioritize them.

  • Certain and low risk ideas: Can be done by engineers quickly without designs. We scheduled these tasks first so that we can have more lead time for design.
  • High risk and uncertain ideas: Needs design and testing.

De-risking the most costly change

One of the riskier, more uncertain ideas we had was to allow students to simulate different profiles in one screen. This enables power users to test chances on one screen and allows other users to see the power user use case. Seeing how chances change with different profiles also helps with understanding.

We tested prototype v1 where students can add “goal profiles”, see chances for their schools, and also see how their chances breakdown for each individual school.

The idea clicked, but the naming did not. When asked ”what do you think would happen if you click on add goal profile”, most students were not sure what it was.

We also found that students would typically ask questions like “How does race affect the admissions process?” or “How do extracurricular activities impact chancing?”. This is when I realized students think of their profile not as whole as we do, but as individual components, because the individual components are what they can improve on.

This finding inspired me to create a solution that shows the profile by section. Students can see their platform profile values and simulated values side by side and clearly see the difference.

In the final version, we also added capabilities to see the chancing difference and optimized the discoverability of the section-based breakdown on the right hand side.

Impact

After launch, we observed that:

  • Conversion rate from signup to adding a school increased by 20%
  • Retention (8 week average) increased by 20%

In addition, we established a way to dramatically decrease time-to-value. Instead of spending 15–30 minutes to see any results, anonymous users who find us online can immediately see how our chancing calculator works. Instead of inputting their entire profile, they can view chances calculated based on default values and start experimenting right away. Because of the instant value, the chancing calculator was used for growing new accounts later.

Reflections

No matter how familiar we are with a product, we are not the users. Talking to students really helped me think outside the box, break my existing mental model about how the profile works, and find a solution that truly resonates with users.
