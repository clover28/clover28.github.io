Overview

In this project, I designed College Hub, a web-based college search product that greatly simplifies the way high school students in the US pick colleges. It became the core retention engine of the entire college application guidance platform.
I kept learning about how users interact with it and iterating on it after the initial launch. I also put its components into our design system to help the design team work faster.

Context

Building a guidance platform from scratch

In 2020, CollegeVine decided to build a SaaS platform that can provide college application guidance at scale, from scratch.

This platform needs to solve every major job-to-be-done in the application journey and keep students engaged. Among the jobs-to-be-done, building a list of colleges to apply to is one of the most important ones. My team was tasked with creating a state-of-the-art solution for this job.

Weekly build-measure-learn cycles

Because we were starting from the ground up, the team were working in full build-measure-learn mode, shipping new experiments and improvements on a weekly basis. I frequently brainstormed with my team, made roadmap suggestions, designed in very short turn-around time (2‚Äì4 days), and monitored results through usage data, screen recordings, NPS score, and user conversations. However, when it is appropriate for the project, I never hesitated to advocate for more user research and user testing to de-risk our decisions.

North-star: Retention

Since the top priority was to prove the value of the guidance platform, retention was our north star. This goal determined that we needed to become the place where students would stay to work on their college list.

How might we become the place students build their college list?

The discovery

To put ourselves in high school students‚Äô shoes, I led the user interview effort. Together with the product manager, I interviewed over 20 students and collected their real college lists. I analyzed qualitative data with affinity diagramming and painted a clear picture of the current college search process:

Big picture

Students usually need to find 20‚Äì30 colleges and narrow the list down to the 10‚Äì15 they eventually would apply to. Because there are over 5000 colleges in the US and multitude of factors to consider, this process involves significant research and iteration.

Tools

Most students do online search and use spreadsheets. Online search to research colleges, and spreadsheets to organize the research.

Pain points

1. Information is scattered across hundreds of college websites that have very different navigation structures.

2. Organizing the research in spreadsheets is a tedious, manual task, exacerbated by the fact that information is hard to find and there are tens of factors to consider.

Competitors

Several college search websites that were mentioned in the interviews, but after a closer analysis, I found that they are limited because:

  1. They only have limited data on colleges. This means students still need to do research elsewhere.
  1. They do not allow students to organize their research findings.

Due to these limitations, these websites are used only as references occasionally, but not where students would actually build their college lists.

Even though building a college list is a key job in the application process, it is still under-served by the market and students are still doing it manually with tedious online research and spreadsheet making.

Generating hypotheses

I summarized the insights into 2 design principles we can further validate:

1. Everything in one place

Instead of searching for scattered information on multiple websites with many tabs open

2. Easily customizable output

Each student care about different aspects of colleges. They need to compare colleges and organize their research based on what they care about. Competitors do not support that.

Explorations and Solutions

All data in one place

Scalable, scannable filters

With time as the biggest constraint, we decided to build upon an existing tech demo that has a side filter. With the breadth of data, CollegeVine can meet students' long-tail search needs and even help them realize their needs. The challenge is that the number of filters can be overwhelming.

I organized the filters into semantic categories and prioritized them for students so that it is easy to scan and digest. I also defined a scalable UI system for the filters, making it easy to add in new ones. When we added 20 more filterable data points in , average meaningful actions per user grew by 25%. This showed ‚Äúall data in one place‚Äù was working and that the large number of filters were still easy enough to use.

Align the team with user insights

One conflict between product and engineering was whether and when we should add majors in the filter. Adding in major data has been pushed off because it would take weeks, if not months. However, based on the user interviews, I was confident that majors is an essential part in college search and a must-have for our retention goal. Eventually, the team was convinced by the user insight and decided to spend the time to collect the data.

I also convinced the engineers to create a custom UI for the major filter because we found that searching for colleges by major is more complex than we imagined. Students can be undecided between several majors and looking for colleges that have one of these majors, or they may want to enter a college with all of these majors so that they can decide later. They can also be looking to double major at one college. Knowing the different use cases, I created a solution that allows students to search for colleges with multiple majors. Students can also switch between ‚Äúand‚Äù and ‚Äúor‚Äù logic.

When we added the major filter in, average meaningful actions per user went up; NPS scores and retention also went up significantly for this cohort.

All interactions in one place

In addition to centralizing useful information, we want to make it a streamlined, uninterrupted one-tab experience, as opposed to the multi-tab, multi-platform research process students normally need to go through. We made the conscious decision to not open up a new tab when users click on a college and added a clear back button to guide users back to search mode.

When we saw students get into flow state on our platform and explore hundreds of colleges in 2-hour long sessions, we knew that we were heading in the right direction.

Easily customizable output

Displaying the search results is another important problem to solve in the design process. I started by exploring different ways of displaying colleges with quick, lo-fi mocks and explaining their traits to the team.

list

table

cards

histogram

After weighing the pros and cons, we decided to move forward with the table view. Compared to other views, the table makes it really easy to compare colleges, sort by any criteria you want, and add any criteria you care about in a scalable way. It also fits the mental model of users who are already using spreadsheets. It feels more rigid visually, but this was a tradeoff we were willing to make.

As for the organization features, we wanted to allow students to add data columns they care about and order them. I considered 2 interaction models: in-table and modal. We moved forward with a 1-modal approach because: 1) It allows users to organize multiple data points more easily; 2) It takes less time to build because we have all the existing UI components.

Impact

Users who used College Hub had a much higher retention rate than platform users who did not use this feature. It became the core retention engine and main feature of the entire platform. It was later used as a base for other important features such as calculating admissions chances and tracking deadlines.

Not only has it played an important role in the business, it truly has helped millions of students find colleges.

Learnings and Iterations

After the initial launch, I kept learning about how users interact with College Hub and made improvements throughout the years when they fit into the business strategy.

Discoverability

One important lesson learned is that a useful feature is useless if people cannot discover it.

After the initial launch, we found that the engagement rate on the customization feature is not as high as we had hoped. We conducted usability tests on the live site with users, and found that it was hard for users to discover the button and hard for them to guess correctly what the button does. However, they were very excited when shown this capability.

This showed that:

  1. The button is not prominent enough visually.
  1. The copy is not clear enough.
  1. There can be more onboarding effort. Because this is a feature unique to us, users are not expecting it or looking for it.

I made the following improvements:

  1. Use a primary button instead of a secondary one
  1. Make the copy clearer and add an ‚Äúadd‚Äù icon
  1. Develop in-context onboarding to draw users‚Äô attention to the feature

Similarly, through watching screen recordings, I found that many users do not know to scroll horizontally and can miss certain information, especially when their browser does not show the scroll bar by default. To aid the discoverability of more information, I added horizontal scroll arrows and a shadow to indicate the continuity of space.

These learnings have taught me to not only make sure the interface has appropriate visual emphasis, but also always double check discoverability and onboarding when launching new features.

Enabling the full experience on mobile

When we initially launched the product, we prioritized desktop because we found that students mostly do their college research on computers. Mobile still was not a priority years after, since the ratio of desktop users remained much higher than mobile. Part of the reason was the nature of college application as a serious task. However, I was certain that we were losing mobile usage simply due to the poor experience on mobile.

Fortunately, we had some downtime between roadmap items and the team agreed it was a perfect time to squeeze in some mobile UX improvements. The challenge was how to organize the many features we have in a much tighter space.

I approached this challenge mainly from two dimensions: information hierarchy and familiar patterns. First I listed all the elements on the page from the most important to the least. Then I conducted UI research to see how other ‚Äúsearch‚Äù products organize their features.

I explored a number of solutions with different UI components and arrangements. After evaluating each option from the perspectives of logic and visual standpoints, I found that grouping page-level actions on top and sorting and filtering at the bottom makes sense logically and visually. Even though sorting and filtering are at the bottom, they are in a familiar pattern that users can quickly recognize.

After launch, the conversion rate from sign up to completing a meaningful action increased by 71%. Retention rate on mobile also became higher long-term.
